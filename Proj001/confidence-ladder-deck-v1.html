The Confidence Ladder

Climb from guesswork to proof in your game

A studio-side toolkit that turns “we think our game is fair” into “we can prove it” — using AI/ML where it earns its keep, and mathematics where the stakes demand certainty. No prior data-science knowledge needed — we explain every idea in plain language first.

Slide purpose & context. The cold-open. The room mixes studio engineers, LiveOps leads, designers, and producers — many of whom are not data scientists. The job of this slide is to plant the central metaphor (a ladder of confidence), signal this is a studio-side talk (how we know our own game behaves), and promise — explicitly — that no math background is required. Hold ~30 seconds.

The business problem · 01

Two things that are both true at once.

Your dashboard says the economy is healthy. Your community says the drop rates are rigged, the ladder is full of cheaters, and that ban was unfair.

The operator’s real question: “Is my game behaving as I designed it — and how strongly can I actually know that it is?” Today, the honest answer is usually: we think so.

Your telemetry

“Economy healthy ✅”

Aggregate metrics inside normal bands. Faucets and sinks balanced. No alert fired.

Your community

“It’s rigged.”

Threads on impossible drop rates, win-trading rings, and a wrongful ban — none of which your dashboard can confirm or deny.

Slide purpose & context. Establish the business problem as tension, not a feature list. The most relatable operator pain isn't "we have cheaters" — it's the gap between what the instruments say and what reality is. This dramatizes that gap so the rest of the talk has something to close.

Headline — "Two things both true at once." The dashboard and the angry players aren't contradicting each other; both can be right because the dashboard measures the wrong altitude of certainty. Aggregate health can be green while a targeted exploit, a collusion ring, or a tampered drop table runs underneath.

Left card — "Economy healthy ✅." Today's observability: aggregate, lagging, threshold-based. Not wrong — just blind to what players are shouting about.

Right card — "It's rigged." The three perennial accusations — bad odds, win-trading, unfair bans — chosen because each maps to a later rung (odds → Rung 3; rings → Rung 1; unfair ban → the "attach a reason" theme).

Strip — "we think so." Land this hard; pause after it. The whole talk replaces "we think so" with calibrated, defensible answers. Reader note: this exact phrase is paid off on the closing slide ("we can prove it").

The business problem · 02  ·  why it costs real money

Four places the gap shows up on the P&L.

Revenue erosion

Cheats, fraud, RMT

Refunds, chargebacks, gold-farming, and paying players who quit a cheater-ridden ladder. Direct, measurable loss.

Trust & retention

Churn from doubt

When players stop believing the odds or the rank are fair, they stop spending — long before they post about it.

Regulatory exposure

Loot-box scrutiny

Odds-disclosure mandates and gambling reclassification mean you may need casino-grade audit trails — not optional.

Operational cost

Analyst hours & disputes

Every alert with no rationale is an investigation. Every “I was robbed” is a CS escalation. It scales badly.

Every rung in this talk is accountable to these four. If a technique doesn’t move one of them, it’s a science project — not an investment.

Slide purpose & context. Convert slide 2's tension into money, because the room includes the people who fund the work. Four cards, four distinct cost centers; we refer back to these when deciding which rungs are worth building.

Card 1 — Revenue erosion. The most concrete: cheating/fraud/RMT cause refunds, chargebacks, and churn of the exact players who pay. Lead ROI argument; maps to Rung 1.

Card 2 — Trust & retention. The silent one: players who doubt fairness spend less before they ever complain, so it never shows up as a ticket. Maps to Rungs 3–4.

Card 3 — Regulatory exposure. Board-level risk: loot-box-as-gambling and odds-disclosure rules. If they land you need auditable RNG anyway, so early building is a moat. Maps to Rung 3 / optional Rung 4.

Card 4 — Operational cost. Felt daily: alerts without rationale = investigation hours; disputes without evidence = escalations. Maps to the cross-cutting "every signal ships a reason" theme.

Strip — "or it's a science project." Credibility line: the techniques are judged against these four outcomes, nothing else. Pre-empts the "tech for tech's sake" skeptic and sets up the honest "you may not need all of it" later.

Baseline · 02  ·  the gaps that hit the P&L

Good tools. Four blind spots.

Blind spot · reactive

You find out after the damage

The dupe is noticed when prices crater; the bot ring when reports pile up. Tools say “something broke,” rarely “here’s the rule that allowed it.”

Blind spot · evasion

Cheats tune just under the line

A fixed threshold is a target. Adaptive cheats sit one notch below every rule and pass clean.

Blind spot · relational

Per-account rules can’t see rings

Collusion and win-trading are invisible one account at a time — each member looks ordinary. The signal is in the relationships.

Blind spot · self-trust

You assume your own pipeline is honest

Is prod running the odds you configured? The binary you shipped? Usually there’s no artifact that proves it — it’s assumed.

And a fifth, cutting across all of them: most alerts don’t localize the root cause. “Metric X spiked” still costs an analyst hours. Every rung that follows hands back a reason, not just an alarm.

Slide purpose & context. The pivot. Having honoured the baseline, name precisely where it goes blind — and tie each blind spot to a cost from slide 3. These four gaps are exactly what the four rungs solve, so this is the structural hinge of the deck. Tone: diagnostic, not gleeful.

Blind spot 1 — Reactive. Today's analytics are lagging: you learn of the dupe after the economy absorbed it. → motivates Rung 2 (prove the exploit can't exist before launch).

Blind spot 2 — Evasion. A published/guessable threshold is something an adversary optimises against; the evasive cheat tunes every stat just under the line. → motivates Rung 1's anomaly detection (model "normal," not a fixed line).

Blind spot 3 — Relational. Per-account scoring structurally can't see collusion — each colluder looks normal; only the graph of who-plays-whom reveals the ring. → motivates Rung 1's graph beat.

Blind spot 4 — Self-trust. The most under-served gap: studios verify the client exhaustively but assume their own backend is honest. → motivates Rung 3.

Strip — the fifth gap: no localization. The unifying complaint: alarms without rationale. State the principle every following rung obeys — "hand back a reason, not just an alarm." Reader note: this thread connects otherwise different techniques (feature attribution on models, the implicated subgraph, the named tampered record).

The idea

One idea: match the strength of your evidence to the stakes.

Every signal sits on a ladder — from a probabilistic suspicion to a mathematical proof. Higher rungs cost more and prove more.

You don’t need every rung. The skill isn’t climbing as high as possible — it’s knowing which rung your problem needs, and stopping there. Pick and choose.

Slide purpose & context. The keystone — the one image the audience carries out. Everything before built the need; everything after tours these four rungs. Spend ~90 seconds. The right-edge rail is a miniature of this exact ladder.

Headline — "match evidence strength to stakes." The thesis. Not "use more math" — calibrate. This is the spine and the line that makes the talk defensible to a skeptic.

The ladder, bottom to top — read each with its strength label.

  • Rung 1 · Detect — "Probabilistic." Produces ranked suspicions with reasons; false positives by design → feeds human review, never auto-bans.
  • Rung 2 · Verify rules — "Proof of the rules." Proves no reachable state in your specified ruleset breaks an invariant. Real proof — over the rules as written, not the shipped code.
  • Rung 3 · Verify pipeline — "Cryptographic proof." A tamper provably fails verification — a hard true positive, not a suspicion.
  • Rung 4 · Expose — "Optional, outward." Publishing a rung-2/3 artifact so an outsider can re-check; as strong as the artifact beneath it.

Strip — "you don't need every rung." The single most trust-building sentence; say it early and mean it. It frames the rest of the talk as "here are your options," not "here's a mandate" — what a senior engineer respects.

Rung 1 · Detect  ·  Technique 1 of 2: anomaly detection, in plain English

Spotting the fake without ever being shown one.

🏦✍️
Imagine…

A bank teller who has handled thousands of real signatures. Nobody ever taught them what a forgery looks like — but when a fake slides across the counter, it just feels wrong. The slant, the pressure, the speed: each could be fine alone, but together they don’t match what “real” feels like.

1

The computer studies normal players — thousands of them — across many traits at once: aim steadiness, reaction time, how often they land a perfect shot.

2

It builds a sense of “what normal feels like” — not a rulebook, a feel for the whole crowd.

3

Anything that doesn’t fit gets flagged — even when every single trait, on its own, looks perfectly legal.

The gist: instead of a checklist of what’s bad (which a cheat can dodge), it learns what’s normal and notices what’s off — weighing lots of small oddities together. The fancy name is anomaly detection.

Slide purpose & context. The first "explain like I'm five" slide. Goal: a non-technical attendee leaves able to describe anomaly detection in one sentence to a colleague. The rail's bottom rung (cool blue) is now lit. Spend ~75 seconds; let the analogy breathe before the mechanism.

Analogy — the bank teller. Chosen deliberately: it captures the two ideas that matter — (1) you learn from normal examples, never from labelled fakes, and (2) the giveaway is the combination of traits, not any single one. Tell it as a story, not a definition. Alternative analogies if the room prefers: recognising a stranger's footsteps on your stairs; a parent knowing their child is "off" without a thermometer.

Mechanism, three steps (plain). (1) Study many normal players across many traits. (2) Build a feel for normal — emphasise it's a learned feel, not a written rule. (3) Flag what doesn't fit, even when each trait alone is legal. Keep the language exactly this plain; resist saying "z-score," "distribution," or "feature vector" out loud here.

Gist line — the payoff + the real name. Only after the intuition lands do we attach the term "anomaly detection." This ordering — intuition first, jargon last — is the whole point of the slide. Reader note: the key contrast to seed here, paid off on the next-but-one slide, is "checklist of bad (dodgeable) vs. sense of normal (not dodgeable)."

Rung 1 · Detect  ·  Technique 2 of 2: graph analysis, in plain English

The conspiracy you can’t see one suspect at a time.

🕵️🧵📌
Imagine…

A detective’s corkboard. Interview any one suspect and they seem ordinary. But pin up who calls whom, who meets whom, and stretch string between them — and a tight little cluster lights up. The plot was never in one person. It was in the connections.

1

Draw a dot for every player, and a line between two players each time they play each other.

2

Cheating partners light up as a tight knot — they face each other far more than chance would ever explain.

3

The shape gives them away, even though each player’s own stats look completely normal.

The gist: some cheating only exists between people — win-trading, boosting, fraud rings. Looking at the map of who-plays-whom reveals the group. The fancy name is a graph; the smart version that learns these patterns is a graph neural network (GNN).

Slide purpose & context. The second plain-language explainer, paired with the first because both live on Rung 1. Goal: the audience understands that some abuse is relational — invisible per-account, obvious as a structure. Rail stays on Rung 1.

Analogy — the detective's corkboard. The string-and-pins image is near-universal from crime drama. It carries the exact insight: one suspect looks innocent; the web of connections reveals the conspiracy. This maps perfectly to win-trading, where each colluder's individual record is unremarkable.

Mechanism, three steps (plain). (1) A dot per player, a line each time two play each other — this is literally what "a graph" means; say so, demystifying the word. (2) Colluders form a tight knot because they face each other far more than chance. (3) The shape betrays them despite normal individual stats. Avoid "node embeddings," "topology," "adjacency" out loud.

Gist line — names attached last. Introduce "graph" as simply "a map of connections," then note the learning version is a "graph neural network (GNN)." Reader note: in the live demo we use transparent graph analysis (connected clusters) rather than a trained GNN so it runs in seconds; the structure it finds is exactly what a production GNN would learn — say this when the demo appears so nobody thinks the demo is the full production system.

Rung 1 · Detect  ·  closing the loop: the analyst copilot

A detection is useless until a human can act on it fast.

🧑‍⚖️🤖📋
Imagine…

Your fraud-and-cheat team drowns in flags — thousands a day, each just a number. Now imagine a sharp junior analyst who never tires: for every flag they read the player’s history, write a plain-English “here’s why this looks like win-trading,” attach the evidence, and sort the pile by severity. They don’t pass judgement — they make the human judge ten times faster.

1

It reads the messy evidence — the score, the feature that fired, the player’s recent matches and trades, the implicated ring.

2

It writes the case in plain language — “flagged for reaction times too consistent across 40 matches; trades only with 3 linked accounts.”

3

It triages and drafts — ranks by severity, drafts the case note, suggests next steps. The human decides.

The gist: a large language model (the tech behind chat assistants) turns raw “score + features” into a reviewer-ready case file. It’s the force-multiplier for the human-review bottleneck every detection system eventually hits — and the natural generative-AI hook in the stack. It explains and accelerates; it never auto-bans.

Slide purpose & context. The close of Rung 1 — and the answer to "so a model flagged 4,000 sessions… now what?" Every probabilistic detector creates a human-review bottleneck; this slide shows the LLM copilot as the thing that makes that human fast, consistent, and defensible. It is also the most natural generative-AI / Amazon Bedrock hook in the whole talk, which matters for an AWS audience. Rail stays on Rung 1 (cool blue). Spend ~60 seconds.

Headline — "useless until a human can act on it fast." The motivating truth: detection volume without triage just moves the bottleneck. Rung 1 catches more (slides 8–10) — which would drown a review team without this. The copilot is what makes higher recall operationally affordable.

Analogy — the tireless junior analyst. Deliberately chosen to bound the claim: a junior analyst prepares the case, a senior human decides. This keeps the copilot firmly on the right side of the honesty line (detect → explain → human review → remedy; never auto-ban). Tell it as "ten times faster," not "replaces the team."

Mechanism, three steps (plain). (1) Reads the messy evidence — score, the feature that fired, recent matches/trades, the implicated ring from the graph beat. (2) Writes the case in plain language a reviewer (or an appeals process) can act on. (3) Triages by severity and drafts the note; the human decides. Avoid "prompt," "context window," "RAG," "inference" out loud — say "reads," "writes," "sorts."

Gist line — name + boundary + AWS hook. Attach the term "large language model / generative AI" last, exactly as with the other techniques. Restate the boundary: it explains and accelerates, never auto-bans. Reader note & AWS hook: in production this is an LLM on Amazon Bedrock ingesting the detection's "score + features + subgraph + history" and emitting a plain-language case note; it ties the probabilistic detections (anomaly + graph) into the human remedy workflow. This is the slide where the abstract's "explain why" promise becomes a concrete tool.

Rung 2 · Verify rules  ·  the technique, in plain English

A tireless rule-lawyer who checks every possible move.

📜⚖️♾️
Imagine…

Your playtesters try thousands of things hoping to break the game. Now imagine instead a tireless logician who reads the rulebook and checks every move sequence that could ever happen — and either hands you the exact recipe that breaks it, or proves, with certainty, that nothing can.

1

Write the shop & crafting rules as precise logic — what buying, selling, and crafting each do to a player’s gold and items.

2

State the promise you want kept — e.g. “no loop of buying, selling, and crafting can leave a player with the same items but more gold.”

3

The solver searches all possibilities at once — not by trying them one by one, but by reasoning — and reports the exact exploit loop or proves there is none.

The gist: playtesting is “we tried a lot and didn’t break it.” This is “we proved it can’t break.” Like proving a Sudoku has exactly one answer — without trying every grid. The fancy name is Automated Reasoning (formal methods).

Slide purpose & context. The plain-language explainer for the most unfamiliar — and most differentiated — technique. The leap a lay audience must make: from "test a lot and hope" to "prove it can't happen." The rail's second rung (teal) lights. This is the conceptual heart of the talk; spend ~90 seconds.

Analogy — the tireless rule-lawyer / logician. Contrasts the familiar (playtesters trying things) with the new (a logician checking every possible sequence by reasoning, not by trial). The two outcomes are the point: it either hands you the exact exploit recipe or proves none exists. Both are valuable.

Mechanism, three steps (plain). (1) Write the rules as precise logic (what each action does to gold/items/stats). (2) State the promise to keep ("never end with more gold than earned"). (3) The solver searches all possibilities by reasoning, not one-by-one — and returns a loophole or a proof. Emphasise "all at once, by reasoning" — that's why it's certainty, not sampling. Avoid "SMT solver," "satisfiability," "state space" out loud.

Gist line — the Sudoku image + the name. "Proving a Sudoku has exactly one answer without trying every grid" is the cleanest everyday image of proof-vs-search. Attach the name "Automated Reasoning (formal methods)" last. Reader note & honest ceiling: the proof is over the rules as written, not the shipped code — mention this here lightly; it's stated fully on the credibility slide. AWS hook: Bedrock Guardrails' Automated Reasoning checks; AWS's formal-methods heritage (Zelkova, s2n). Verify current naming before presenting.

Rung 3 · Verify pipeline  ·  the technique, in plain English

The sealed envelope you can’t secretly swap.

✉️🔒🔍
Imagine…

Before the draw, the game writes the result on a card, seals it in an envelope, and hands you a tamper-proof fingerprint of what’s inside. You make your move. Then the envelope is opened — and you check the fingerprint still matches. The game could not have changed the card after seeing what you did.

1

A “fingerprint” is published first. Change even one letter inside, and the fingerprint changes completely — and you can’t work backwards from it to peek.

2

The outcome is locked in before you act — and depends partly on a number you contribute, so the house can’t cherry-pick it.

3

Anyone can re-check later. If a result was edited afterward, the fingerprint won’t match — a guaranteed catch, not a guess.

The gist: a one-way “fingerprint” (a hash) lets you lock something in publicly without revealing it, then prove later it was never changed. The pattern is commit-reveal; stronger cousins are VRF and attestation.

Slide purpose & context. Plain-language explainer for the cryptographic rung — the one that sounds most intimidating and is actually the most intuitive once anchored to a sealed envelope. Goal: a lay attendee understands "you can lock something in publicly without revealing it, and prove later it wasn't changed." Rail's third rung (gold) lights.

Analogy — the sealed envelope / sealed prediction. Universally understood (sealed bids, a magician's sealed prediction, sealed exam papers). It carries all three ideas: commit before the action, can't change after seeing the move, anyone can check at reveal.

Mechanism, three steps (plain). (1) A "fingerprint" (hash) is published first — explain its two magic properties in plain words: any tiny change flips it completely, and you can't reverse it to peek inside. (2) The outcome is locked before the player acts, and depends partly on a number the player contributes, so the house can't cherry-pick. (3) Anyone can re-check later; an edited result won't match — a guaranteed catch. Avoid "SHA-256," "preimage resistance," "nonce" as bare terms — describe them.

Gist line — names last. Introduce "hash = one-way fingerprint," then the pattern "commit-reveal," then note stronger cousins: VRF (randomness the server can't bias) and attestation (proof the running program is the one you shipped). Reader note & the crucial ceiling (stated fully two slides on): this proves the result wasn't changed after the fact — it does NOT by itself prove the odds were fair. Plant that gently here; don't oversell.

The credibility check  ·  what “proof” does and doesn’t mean

Be precise about what each proof actually proves.

TechniqueWhat it really provesWhat people assume it provesThe gap to name out loud
Commit-revealThe outcome was locked in before you acted.“The odds are fair.”You can honestly seal an envelope drawn from a rigged deck. It kills only the “changed-it-after” trick.
Automated ReasoningNo legal play breaks the rule — in the written rules.“The shipped game has no exploit.”Proof is over the rulebook, not the actual code that shipped.
AttestationProduction is running this exact program.“The game’s logic is correct.”Proves which program runs, not that the program is right.

Why a player would still trust it: they don’t trust your claim — they re-run the check. But it only ever covers the narrow thing the proof is about. Disclosure, governance, and audit bridge the rest — not the math.

Row 1 — Commit-reveal. Proves the outcome was predetermined, not fair. The plain-language version of the ceiling planted on slide 13: you can honestly seal an envelope drawn from a rigged deck. The takeaway that matters Monday  ·  you don’t need all of it

Don’t climb for its own sake. Climb to your stakes.

Answer five questions about your game. The answers tell you which rungs to build — and which to skip.

Single-player, no money, no ladder? Rung 1, if anything. Real money on random outcomes in a regulated market? You’ll want all four — and you’d have needed them anyway.

  • 1Does an adaptive adversary attack you at scale? → Rung 1
  • 2Would a single economy exploit be catastrophic? → Rung 2
  • 3Could an insider or bad deploy alter outcomes? → Rung 3
  • 4Does an outsider (regulator, player, partner) need to verify? → Rung 4
  • 5Does real money, rank, or regulation ride on the result? → climb higher

Slide purpose & context. The single most credible — and most useful — slide. After four impressive rungs, explicitly tell people not to build all of them. This is the "real value add, on Monday" moment: a portable decision rule applied to their own game in ten minutes. Saying "you don't need all of it" earns trust on everything you do recommend.

Headline — "climb to your stakes, not for its own sake." The thesis as advice: the skill is calibration, not maximalism. Separates this from a vendor pitch.

The five questions (read each as "if yes → build this rung").

  • Q1 adaptive adversary at scale → Rung 1. Almost everyone with a live multiplayer or monetized game says yes — why Rung 1 is near-universal.
  • Q2 single economy exploit catastrophic → Rung 2. Yes for tradeable economies, gacha currencies, marketplaces; no for cosmetic-only.
  • Q3 insider / bad deploy could alter outcomes → Rung 3. Yes wherever outcomes carry monetary or competitive value.
  • Q4 an outsider must verify → Rung 4. Yes only under regulation, on-chain, or explicit competitive-integrity guarantees.
  • Q5 money / rank / regulation rides on it → climb higher. The master switch: raises the whole stakes profile and justifies the proof-grade rungs.

Strip — the two extremes. Make it concrete: single-player, non-monetized, non-competitive → Rung 1 at most (often nothing); real-money random-outcome game in a regulated market → all four, and you'd have needed casino-grade audit anyway.
